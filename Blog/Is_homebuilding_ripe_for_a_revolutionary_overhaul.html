Is homebuilding ripe for a revolutionary overhaul?



The current homebuilding industry, facing supply chain delays, inefficiencies, and sustainability issues, has never been more exposed to a wave of transformations than in today’s fast-changing, tech-oriented world.



In the United States and elsewhere in the world, global supply chain delays continue to plague the homebuilding industry.

Housing market research firm Zonda highlights that 90% of American builders surveyed point to supply issues impacting their business, with factors like surging demand for new housing as well as factory and transportation hub closures due to COVID-19 coming together to cause home construction delays and material shortages.

What to expect from these problems but delayed openings and prices on new construction getting driven up? Back in November 2021, the average cost of a new US home saw a 19% hike year over year, or a whopping $416,900. Construction’s aggregate cost of construction also rose by 21% year over year, noted the National Association of Home Builders.

These trends appear to mirror what’s happening elsewhere.

Traditional homebuilding under scrutiny

Homebuilding faces common issues ranging from the lack of knowledge transparency in the architecture, engineering, and construction (AEC) space, along with existing inefficiencies on skilled labor, on which the industry is heavily dependent.

Dominant construction materials like concrete and steel lead to high carbon emissions. Some 39% of CO2 levels worldwide, for instance, are generated by the built environment and construction, according to the World Green Building Council.

Despite the surge in e-commerce adoption, there’s no real linkage between it and homebuilding.

Yet there are pockets of hope and progress across the sector, as mediated by technology and innovations around material use and processes.



Prefab in focus

House construction is constantly evolving globally. Prefabricated or prefab homes today have become an attractive option for budding homeowners in their bid for a cheaper, more sustainable home built in a shorter time.

In markets like the Philippines, prefab housing is catching on if the demand is any indicator. With house components already made, builders simply assemble the pieces on-site like a puzzle, cutting construction time short and eliminating the need to reinforce or lay down hollow blocks and apply cement.

Prefab houses are also built based on standards set by existing laws. With countries like the Philippines, India, and similar markets prone to natural disasters, they are designed to withstand the strongest quakes or monsoon rains.

The rise of automation

Prefab materials and assemblies appear to be part of a bigger phenomenon called industrialized construction (IC). IC combines several technology advancements -- mechanizes and automates -- and incorporates them into the construction process to transform design and manufacturing.

Industrializing construction entails a number of goals:

  • Increase labor productivity
  • Substitute labor-intensive processes with machines
  • Fast-track the rate of construction
  • Commission new projects more rapidly
  • Slash costs
  • Enhance overall quality and sustainability
  • Enable affordable customizations

It’s estimated that by 2035, the majority of buildings will be built using IC as construction’s sophisticated design and manufacturing’s expertise in mass production meet halfway.

There is a vast room to do this, as the construction sector is among the least digitized today, racked with repetitive, labor-intensive processes. A McKinsey study showed that large initiatives usually extend 20% beyond the initial completion date and go up to 80% over budget!



Homebuilding in web3

Another endlessly enticing prospect: designing and building homes on web 3.0, where technologies such as cloud computing, blockchain, and artificial intelligence (AI) synergize to take the built environment at an unprecedented industrial-revolutionary scale.

Web3 is the name used to refer to a new kind of internet service built using decentralized blockchains, the shared ledger systems used by cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin.

Proponent Packy McCormick defines it as “the internet owned by the builders and users, orchestrated with tokens.”

The various forms of web3 materializing in the near future -- including decentralized social networks, play-to-earn (P2E) video games rewarding players with tokens, and NFT platforms for buying and selling digital assets -- are anticipated to transform homebuilding, upend its traditional gatekeepers, and usher in a middleman-free digital economy.

Via novel technologies that augment, a mix of gamification and time-tested AEC logics has also become a viable way to DIY home design and building, effectively democratizing the project.

Smart contracts are another part of the big picture. These are programs stored on a blockchain to automate the execution of agreements, such that participants (e.g., the contractor and homebuying client) can be certain of expected outcomes; the transaction cannot be changed and only parties given permission can see the results.

Accuracy, trust and transparency, and security are just some benefits of smart contracts.

Homebuilding globally is undergoing drastic changes and transformations. For a number of insiders and new key players, it’s long overdue and only fitting that its revolution is underway.
